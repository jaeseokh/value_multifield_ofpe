7  Bayesian Framework

1. Farmer’s Decision Problem under Uncertainty

Farmers choose nitrogen (N) without knowing the true yield response function.
Two empirical realities constrain that decision:

  1. OFPE experiments are highly noisy
    Spatial variability, year effects, and management heterogeneity produce
    imprecise estimates of the yield–N curve from a single field-year trial
    (Jong 2023; Bullock and al. 2019).

  2. APSIM provides mechanistic structure but lacks field specificity
    Process-based crop models such as APSIM capture biophysical N–water–growth dynamics across weather years (Holzworth and al. 2014),
    but they do not replicate field-level soil heterogeneity or yield levels.

Neither source alone delivers a reliable N recommendation.
This motivates a Bayesian learning structure, in which:

  • APSIM provides a prior belief about yield distributions, and
  • OFPE supplies noisy field-specific evidence that updates the prior.

APSIM and OFPE as Complementary Information Sources

Let \(Y(N)\) be random yield at nitrogen rate \(N\),
and let \(p_y\) and \(p_n\) denote crop and nitrogen prices.

Profit is:

\[ \pi(N) = p_y Y(N) - p_n N. \]

The farmer selects:

\[ N^\star = \arg\max_N \mathbb{E}[\pi(N)]. \]

The difficulty is that the expectation \(\mathbb{E}[Y(N)]\) is unknown.
A rational decision maker therefore operates with a subjective predictive distribution for \(Y(N)\).

2. APSIM: A Prior Distribution

APSIM generates a distribution for yield conditional on nitrogen:

\[ f_{\text{prior}}(y \mid N), \]

with implied prior moments:

\[ \big\{ \mu_{1,0}(N),\ \mu_{2,0}(N),\ \mu_{3,0}(N) \big\}. \]

This prior encodes:

  • long-run climate variability,
  • soil × water × nitrogen dynamics,
  • agronomically plausible curvature and diminishing returns.

But APSIM alone cannot reflect:

  • field-specific yield levels,
  • micro-scale soil variation,
  • management idiosyncrasies.

Thus it is an informed prior, not a prescriptive curve.

3. OFPE: Noisy Field-Specific Evidence

One OFPE field-year produces sample moments at each rate \(N\):

\[ \bar{Y}(N), \quad s^2(N), \quad m_3(N), \]

but these reflect one realization of:

  • weather,
  • soil heterogeneity,
  • field operations.

OFPE therefore provides high-variance evidence that cannot stand alone as an estimator of \(Y(N)\).

4. Bayesian Updating: Constructing the Posterior Yield Distribution

The farmer combines APSIM structure with OFPE evidence through:

\[ f_{\text{post}}(y \mid N) \ \propto\ f_{\text{prior}}(y \mid N) \times L(\text{OFPE data} \mid y, N), \]

where the likelihood is derived from OFPE sample moments.

Posterior mean

Under a normal–normal moment-matching structure:

\[ \mu_{1,\text{post}}(N) = \frac{\tau_0 \mu_{1,0}(N) + \tau_d \bar{Y}(N)} {\tau_0 + \tau_d}, \]

where:

  • \(\tau_0 = 1/\mu_{2,0}(N)\) is prior precision,
  • \(\tau_d = 1/[s^2(N)/n(N)]\) is data precision.

This is classical Bayesian shrinkage:
the posterior pulls noisy OFPE data toward the APSIM expectation,
with the weight determined by the relative variances.

Posterior variance

\[ \mu_{2,\text{post}}(N) = \frac{1}{\tau_0 + \tau_d}. \]

Posterior skewness (third moment)

OFPE rarely identifies skewness reliably from a single season.
Thus the third moment is inherited from APSIM:

\[ \mu_{3,\text{post}}(N) = \mu_{3,0}(N). \]

Posterior predictive distribution

Given the triple of posterior moments
\((\mu_{1,\text{post}},\mu_{2,\text{post}},\mu_{3,\text{post}})\),
we construct the maximum-entropy density:

\[ f_{\text{post}}(y \mid N) \propto \exp(\lambda_1 y + \lambda_2 y^2 + \lambda_3 y^3), \]

solving for \(\lambda\)’s so that the posterior moments match
(Jaynes 1957).

This provides a full predictive distribution, not only mean–variance.

5. Bayesian Optimal Nitrogen Rate

Given the posterior density:

\[ N^\star_{\text{post}} = \arg\max_N \Big\{ p_y \mu_{1,\text{post}}(N) - p_n N \Big\}. \]

Posterior expectations produce:

  • smoother and more stable optimal N curves,
  • less sensitivity to OFPE noise,
  • more economically coherent recommendations.

This contrasts sharply with:

  • data-only EONR, which is highly unstable, and
  • APSIM-only EONR, which ignores field-level evidence.

The key conceptual contribution is:

OFPE is not a stand-alone estimator but an updating mechanism that refines prior knowledge about a specific field.

## 4. Bayesian Updating: Constructing the Posterior Yield Distribution {-}

The farmer combines APSIM structure with OFPE evidence through:

$$
f_{\text{post}}(y \mid N)
\ \propto\
f_{\text{prior}}(y \mid N)
\times
L(\text{OFPE data} \mid y, N),
$$

where the likelihood is derived from OFPE sample moments.

### **Posterior mean** {-}

Under a normal–normal moment-matching structure:

$$
\mu_{1,\text{post}}(N)
=
\frac{\tau_0 \mu_{1,0}(N) + \tau_d \bar{Y}(N)}
     {\tau_0 + \tau_d},
$$

where:

- $\tau_0 = 1/\mu_{2,0}(N)$ is prior precision,  
- $\tau_d = 1/[s^2(N)/n(N)]$ is data precision.

This is classical Bayesian shrinkage:  
the posterior pulls noisy OFPE data toward the APSIM expectation,  
with the weight determined by the relative variances.

### **Posterior variance** {-}

$$
\mu_{2,\text{post}}(N)
=
\frac{1}{\tau_0 + \tau_d}.
$$

### **Posterior skewness (third moment)** {-}

OFPE rarely identifies skewness reliably from a single season.  
Thus the third moment is inherited from APSIM:

$$
\mu_{3,\text{post}}(N) = \mu_{3,0}(N).
$$

Given the triple of posterior moments  
$(\mu_{1,\text{post}},\mu_{2,\text{post}},\mu_{3,\text{post}})$,

\propto
\exp(\lambda_1 y + \lambda_2 y^2 + \lambda_3 y^3),

[@jaynes1957information].

This provides a *full predictive distribution*, not only mean–variance.
